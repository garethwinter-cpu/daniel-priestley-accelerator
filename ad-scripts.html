5 · Declare Your CapacityCommunication Tips · Organic, teaching mode · ~75sRead scriptHide
Communication TipsOrganic, teaching mode~75s

Key drivers: Providing Value · Actionable Script · Perspective Shift · Credibility

ShootOn-screen text card: "OFFICIAL CAPACITY". Teaching tone, like he's giving you a move you can use tonight.

One sentence changes how the market treats you: "I only take on twelve clients a year." Here's how to use it today. Pick the number of clients you can serve where every single one ends up delighted — not just served. A unit of capacity is a unit of delight. Maybe it's twelve. Maybe it's six hundred. Now the uncomfortable bit: say it out loud, in your marketing. Name your bottleneck — onboarding, the way you get the breakthrough, customer success — the reason you can't take everyone even if you wanted to. Why does this work? Because customers don't buy what you want to sell — they buy what other people want to buy. A coach who wants everyone? No pull. A coach with a two-year waiting list? Your first question is "how do I skip the line?" And one rule most people miss: if you can seat 80, market for 100 — because when 20 people get turned away, that's what spreads. Declaring capacity is step one of five. The other four are free at Mindvalley — link below.

Anchored to: "set your official capacity," "a unit of capacity is a unit of delight," the two-year-waitlist coach, "if you've got 80, market for 100."

6 · Signals Before SalesCommunication Tips · Organic + festival b-roll · ~75sRead scriptHide
Communication TipsOrganic + festival b-roll~75s

Key drivers: Providing Value · Storytelling · Curiosity Gap · Credibility

ShootCut to festival crowd on "Glastonbury," then a simple waitlist form on "raise a hand."

Stop selling. You're doing it backwards. First you make your market, then you make your sales. Look at the biggest music festival on earth: for 364 and a half days a year you cannot buy a Glastonbury ticket — you can only register interest. Seven people register for every ticket that exists. Everyone knows it. Then the tickets drop and sell in under an hour. So here's your homework: stop asking strangers to buy. Ask them to raise a hand first — a rolling waitlist, a workshop, a scorecard, an expression of interest. Then be transparent about the tension: "340 registered for 40 places." I did exactly this launching a software company — 5,500 on the waitlist, 1,000 spots, gone in hours, and 4,500 genuinely missed out. But there's a specific ratio of signals to sales you need before you open the doors — get it wrong and the launch flops. I give you the exact ratio, free, at Mindvalley — link below.

Anchored to: Principle 3 (Glastonbury 7:1, rolling waitlist / scorecard / EOI) + the 5,500-person SaaS waitlist.

Shoot notes

How to run the day

  • Film first (lowest lift, pure talking-head): #3 Algorithm, #4 Invisible, #7 Who Pays, #9 Outwork Intelligence — one idea each, no props.
  • Capture on stage / from the event: #2 Airlines vs Rolex and #10 the anthem — both cut cleanly from accelerator footage.
  • Needs a graphic or b-roll: #1 Bananas (supply/demand graph), #5 Capacity (text card), #6 Signals (festival crowd + waitlist form), #7 Who Pays (three faces / three prices).
  • Every script ends the same way — "free class at Mindvalley — link below" — so the destination and CTA never change across the funnel.
  • Before paid media: clear the "7 businesses zero-to-million" and "$1.3M / $10.7M" revenue figures used in #9 and #10.
